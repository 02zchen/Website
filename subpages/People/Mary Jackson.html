Mary Winston Jackson

Early Life

Mary Winston Jackson was born on April 9th, 1921, in Hampton, Virginia. She graduated from high school with highest honors and went on to attend Hampton Institute, from which she graduated with a dual degree in physical sciences and mathematics in 1942. Her path to NASA was not direct. She held several other jobs after her graduation, including being a math teacher in a Maryland high school, a bookkeeper at the National Catholic Community Center in Hampton, and a receptionist and clerk at Hampton Institute’s Health Department. In 1944, she married Levi Jackson, a sailor in the Navy, and had two children: Levi Jackson, Jr. And Carolyn Marie Lewis. Finally, in 1951, she was hired by the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA, later changed to the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, or NASA).

Early Career

Jackson worked in the West Area Computing unit of the Langley Memorial Aeronautical Laboratory (later NASA Langley Research Facility), a segregated unit for female African American mathematicians. In 1953, Jackson began working for engineer Kazimierz Czarnecki conducting experiments in the Supersonic Pressure Tunnel. Jackson gained a lot of hands-on experience from her time working for Czarnecki, and he eventually suggested that she enter a training program to become an engineer herself. Participants in the course had to take graduate-level classes in math and physics taught at Hampton High School. Since the school was segregated, Jackson had to request special permission from the City of Hampton to join the class. Undeterred by the obstacle, she completed the courses and became NASA’s first female African American engineer in 1958.

Late Career

Jackson worked as an engineer at NASA for approximately 20 years, focusing on the boundary layer of air around airplanes. She became frustrated by her slowing promotions and inability to break into management. Realizing that the same was true for the other women at NASA, she took a demotion in 1979 to fill the open position of Langley’s Federal Women’s Program Manager. In that position, she worked to improve opportunities for women at the organization. She retired in 1985 after a long and successful career. Her career was not the only way she helped her community. She served as a Girl Scout troop leader for over thirty years, and in the 70s, she assisted children in the science club at Hampton’s King Street Community Center in building a wind tunnel to conduct experiments. Mary Jackson passed away on February 11, 2005. Her tireless work to break boundaries and uplift women in STEM will never cease to inspire the world.

Connection to DreamPort

It is fitting that Dreamport name a room after Mary Jackson, since they represent similar ideals. Mary Jackson’s work with the Girl Scouts and the community center in her neighborhood exemplifies her dedication to the betterment of her community, more specifically the children in her community. Dreamport demonstrates that same dedication through the opening of their internship program. By taking on high school interns and allowing them to explore their interest in STEM, Dreamport is providing the same learning environment that Jackson provided to the children in the community center.
