Robert Norton Noyce

About

Robert Norton Noyce, nicknamed “the Mayor of Silicon Valley”, was an American physicist, born on December 12, 1927, in Burlington, Iowa. Noyce graduated from Grinnell High School in 1945, and graduated Phi Beta Kappa, with a BA in physics and mathematics at Grinnell College in 1949. He was held back a year in high school, where he was sentenced to a year in prison for stealing a 25-pound pig and roasting it at a school luau. Despite this setback, Noyce later applied for the doctoral program in physics at MIT and received a doctorate in physics in 1953.

Career

After graduating from MIT, Noyce worked as a research engineer at the Philco Corporation in Philadelphia but left in 1956 to join the Shockley Semiconductor Laboratory in Mountain View, California. In 1968, Noyce co-founded Intel with Gordon Moore and Andrew Grove. Noyce was praised and known for his relaxed business ethics and office culture ideals -- he treated employees as family, rewarding and encouraging teamwork and coworker synergy. Noyce was one of the first people to witness and oversee the invention of Ted Hoff’s microprocessor. Noyce suffered from a heart attack at age 62 and passed away at Seton Medical Center in Austin, Texas, on June 3, 1990.

Achievements and Awards

  • National Medal of Technology [1987]
  • Inducted into the US Business Hall of Fame [1989]
  • IEEE Medal of Honor [1978]
  • Franklin Institute's Stuart Ballantine Medal [1966]
  • National Medal of Science [1979]
  • Charles Stark Draper Prize [1989]
