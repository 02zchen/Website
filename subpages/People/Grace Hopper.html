Grace Hopper

About

Grace Hopper was a computer scientist and a US rear admiral. She was the reason for many improvements related to technology, which led to her success and fame.

Education

Grace attended the Hartridge School while she attempted to get into the Vassar College before being rejected at age 16. The following year she attempted again and got in. Here she achieved a bachelor's degree in physics and math. Later on she went to Yale University to work for her master's degree and earned a Ph.D. in mathematics in 1934. Hopper started teaching mathematics at Vassar College one year after entering Yale until she joined the Navy in 1943.

Career

Grace tried to join the Navy during the early stages of World War Two but was denied for a variety of reasons. In 1943 she obtained a leave of absence from Vassar and entered the US Navy Reserve. Grace trained at Smith College and graduated first in her class one year later. After she exited Smith College she was assigned to the Bureau of Ships Computation Project in Harvard where she worked on the Mark I computer with Howard Aiken. Later in 1949, Hopper joined the Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation where she was in team developing the UNIVAC I. This was when Grace started believing in the idea of a programming language that used only English words. Three years later the first paper on compilers was made. Her creation of the linker lead to the beginning of COBOL, which is still the biggest business language.

Legacy

Grace Hopper had a multitude of achievements including creating the first linker leading to the creation of COBOL, becoming a rear admiral in the US Navy, and being on the development team of the Mark I and UNIVAC I computers. The relation that Grace Hopper has to DreamPort is quite deep. She created linkers and helped in the creation of more English based programming languages which helps thousands if not millions of people including the ones in DreamPort to write code faster and more precise. Grace also helped in the development of early computers by being on the Mark I and UNIVAC I teams which helped computers in general get better.
