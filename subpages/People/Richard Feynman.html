Richard Feynman

About

Feynman was born in 1918 in New York City to Lucille née Phillips and Melville Arthur Feynman, who were both Lithuanian Jews. His mother worked as a housekeeper, and his father a sales manager. Feynman’s father heavily influenced his curiosity, by encouraging him to ask questions and challenge orthodox thinking. He was always ready to teach Feynman something new. His mother gave him the sense of humor that he carried throughout his life. He attended Far Rockaway High School, which was also attended by other Nobel laureates. Afterwards, he attended the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, where he changed majors from mathematics to electrical engineering. He then attended Princeton University, scoring a perfect score on the physics section of the entrance examinations.

Career

After the United States was brought into World War II by the Pearl Harbor attack, Feynman was recruited by Robert R. Wilson to work on producing enriched uranium for what would become the Manhattan Project. Early in 1943, Oppenheimer, one of the "fathers of the atomic bomb", convinced Feynman to move to Los Alamos to work on designing and building atomic bombs. There, he helped to develop the Bethe-Feynman Equation to calculate the yield of an atomic bomb, as well as administered the group of human computers in the Theoretical Division. He dedicated his research in physics on the superfluidity of liquid helium and “weak decay”. In 1986, he discovered the cause of the Challenger disaster, which was due to rubber O-rings not expanding in the lower temperatures on the day of the launch.

Achievements and Awards

  • Einstein Award
  • E.O. Lawrence Award
  • Nobel Prize in Physics (1965)
  • Oersted Medal (1972)
  • National Medal of Science (1979)
