Walt Disney

About

Walt Disney was a contributor and pioneer in the field on hand-drawn animation in the early to mid-twentieth-century. He began by working with his partner Ub Iwerks to start a small animation and film studio, mainly focusing on advertising and short films. After some setbacks and failures Walt Disney started what is arguably the most widely recognized and successful animation studio.

Early Life

Walt Disney was born in 1901, in Illinois. Disney lied about his age to join the Red Cross during World War I at the age of sixteen. Upon returning home, Disney started on his path through the world of animation by attending Kansas City Art Institute, where he would meet his future business partner, Ub Iwerks.

Disney and Iwerks started their business careers by opening a small company, which focused on short animated films. They were more common than animated movies because they took less time and were almost non-existent before Disney revolutionized the world of animation with his later feature-film length animations. Disney and Iwerks, however, went bankrupt shortly after beginning their company. Disney persisted in his efforts, and despite a man named Charles Mintz and his wife M.J. Winkler stealing his intellectual properties and animators, Disney continued his career in animation with Iwerks. He came up with the idea for a character named Mickey Mouse on a train, and his cartoon Steamboat Willie cemented him as an acclaimed animator.

Career

Walt Disney’s studio was one of the most innovative of its time. First of all, he created the first color cartoon in 1932, which also happened to be the first cartoon to win an Oscar. His innovation continued, with the release of Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs in 1937, which was the first feature film length animation. His innovation revolutionized animation and cemented it as a style of movie on par with live action, which helped create the volume of animated films we see in theaters today. The 1940s and 1950s brought more success for Disney’s studio, with releases like Cinderella and Peter Pan. Disney even opened a theme park called Disneyland in 1955. Disneyworld would open in 1971, five years after his death.
