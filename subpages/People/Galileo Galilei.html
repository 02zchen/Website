Galileo Galilei

About

Galileo Galilei was born on February 15, 1564, in Pisa, Italy. Many consider Galileo the father of modern science, as he made huge contributions to the fields of philosophy, mathematics, cosmology, astronomy, and physics.

Early Life

He was born the first of the six children of Vincenzo Galilei, a famous musician and a scholar. During his childhood, Galileo was heavily influenced by his pious father and pursued the path of priesthood. However, as a result of his father’s urges, Galileo ended up enrolling at the University of Pisa to study medicine instead. Medicine was not his cup of tea and he soon side-tracked and discovered a love for the sciences. In 1583, he made his first large discovery; he created a ruleset that governed the motion of pendulums. His career advanced further and he became the chair of mathematics at the University of Pisa and Padua from 1589 to 1610.

Achievements

As a polymath, Galileo had major accomplishments in the fields of engineering, astronomy, physics, and mathematics. As an engineer, his most commonly known “invention” is his modification of the telescope (Hans Lippershey invented the first telescope) in 1610. The new-and-improved telescope allowed Galileo to observe the universe on a level unbeknownst to human kind at the time. In the January of 1610, Galileo discovered four “stars” (the four largest moons of Jupiter) orbiting around Jupiter. He published a short treatise titled “Siderius Nuncius” describing his observations of the moons. His later works included observations of Venus, sun spots, and most of the more noticeable stars in the Milky Way. In the field of physics, Galileo was also accredited as being the first to understand the fundamentals of frequency. He discovered that pitch was directly correlated with the spacing between each chisel skip as he dragged a chisel across a rock at various speeds. His contributions to mathematics included a theory named Galileo’s Paradox, which despite being no longer viable, was important in the fact that it provided a way to understand the relationship between transfinite numbers (he stated that they could not be compared).
