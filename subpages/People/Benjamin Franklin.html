Benjamin Franklin

About

Born in Boston, Massachusetts on January 17, 1706, Benjamin Franklin was one of 10 children born to Josiah Franklin and Abiah Folger. At the age of 10, Franklin left school to work for his father. At the age of 12, he became an apprentice to his brother in the printing trade. At the age of 15, he and his brother had created the first truly independent paper in the Colonies, The New-England Courant. With this new paper, Franklin posed his views through a made-up character, Mrs. Dogood, a middle-aged widow. Franklin then left his apprenticeship without his brother’s consent and ran away to Philadelphia.

Life

In Philadelphia, Franklin created Junto, which brings together aspiring artisans and tradesmen to better themselves and the community. After members donated books to read, they soon realized that relying solely on donations was not good enough. Here, Franklin developed the idea of a library subscription, where members pooled money and bought books for everyone to share. This lead to the creation of the Library Company of Philadelphia. After the death of his former employer, he returned to his original trade of Printing. He created two more papers, The Pennsylvania Gazette and the Die Philadelphische Zeitung (a German newspaper). Successful ideas such as Junto, subscriptions, and his book “Poor Richard’s Almanack” led to Benjamin Franklin becoming a renowned person in Pennsylvania.

Works

In 1746, Franklin began exploring the concept of electricity. He constructed a capacitor out of glass and lead, which he used to demonstrate the principle of Conservation of Charge. These findings led to him earning multiple awards, including the Royal Society's Copley Medal, degrees from both Harvard and Yale, and the unit of electric charge being named after him, the franklin. His findings led to his famous kite experiment, where Franklin extracted sparks from the clouds, proving that lightning is electrical.

He also worked in many other fields, including oceanography, meteorology, and refrigeration.

After his scientific works, Franklin began to work with the government. He drafted the Declaration of Independence with four others. He soon became an ambassador to France for the United States. After coming back from France, he became president of the Pennsylvania Abolition Society, eventually becoming the President of Pennsylvania. Because of his popular and meaningful works, Franklin can now be found almost everywhere in modern society, between US Postage, exhibits, awards, and the $100 bill.
